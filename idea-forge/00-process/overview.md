# Process overview — the WHO → WHAT → WHY ladder

The forge validates a **problem**, not a solution. The whole method reduces to:
find the right people, name their real pain, understand why it persists, and
prove it with behavior — not opinions.

## The ladder

1. **WHO** — a specific segment you can find this week. "Everyone" is a fail.
2. **WHAT** — the pain, described as if no solution existed. No features here.
3. **WHY** — the structural root cause (five-whys), and why it's still unsolved.

Only after the ladder holds do you talk value proposition, features, metrics.

## The four tests (every problem must pass)

| Test | Question |
|------|----------|
| **Real** | Does this actually happen, observed in behavior? |
| **Large** | Are there enough people with it? |
| **Significant** | Do they care enough to act? |
| **Urgent** | Is it a now problem, not a someday problem? |

A problem that fails any of the four is not worth building for yet.

## said / did / paid (the only evidence that counts)

- **said** — weak. Opinions, intentions, waitlists, "I'd use that." Easy to
  give, costs nothing, predicts little.
- **did** — moderate. Time or reputation committed: built a workaround,
  downloaded a tool, changed a habit.
- **paid** — strong. Money or a binding commitment: deposit, pre-order, paid
  pilot, signed LOI.

Hierarchy: **paid > did > said.** `said` alone never satisfies validation. The
linter encodes this as the evidence floor.

## Why the linter, not the AI

AI is sycophantic by default and easy to talk into agreeing. A deterministic
linter can't be flattered, can't be argued with, and can't grade its own
homework. Acceptance to `FROZEN` is mechanical. The AI helps you author; the
linter decides.

## The forge as procrastination (the meta-risk)

A perfect forge with zero real briefs through it is theater. Tools and research
aren't validation — only real people changing behavior or paying are. Build
lean, run one real problem through it, expand from friction.
