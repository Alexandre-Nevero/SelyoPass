# Problem validation — Mom Test + JTBD + Five Whys

A synthesis, not one framework. Use the parts that fit; don't recite them.

## The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

Ask questions even your mom can't lie to you about. Anchor every question in
**past behavior**, never hypotheticals or your idea.

- Good: "How do you handle this today? Walk me through the last time."
- Good: "What have you tried? What did it cost you?"
- Bad: "Would you use a tool that…?" (invites a polite yes)
- Bad: "Do you think this is a good idea?" (you're fishing for compliments)

Strip compliments from the data. "Love it" is noise. A workaround they built
last Tuesday is signal.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (Moesta / Christensen)

Find the **struggling moment** — the trigger that makes someone fire their old
solution and hire a new one. Map the four forces:

- **Push** — the pain of the current situation.
- **Pull** — the attraction of a new approach.
- **Anxiety** — fear of the new thing.
- **Habit** — inertia of the old thing.

Demand exists when push + pull beats anxiety + habit. Look for the moment, not
the demographic.

## Five Whys

Drill from symptom to structural root cause. Use cautiously: real causes are
often jointly sufficient, not a single linear chain. Stop when you hit something
structural (an incentive, a system of record, a market structure) and can answer
"why is this still unsolved today?"

## Saturation floor

~10 behaviorally-backed conversations before a pattern is trustworthy (6–10
across 3–5 orgs for B2B). A couple of chats is an anecdote. This is a heuristic,
not law — and it's the *target*. The linter's floor (≥3 behavioral, ≥1 paid)
just stops you freezing on vibes.

## Kill criteria (mechanical disqualifiers)

- The user has the problem but has spent **$0 and 0 hours** on it.
- Unit economics can't support go-to-market.
- The solution would violate a hard regulatory constraint.

Declare your fail-states up front. It forces focus on the riskiest assumption
first instead of polishing the parts that already work.
